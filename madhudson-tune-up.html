What changed

Eight changes, three themes. Nothing looks different on the site — it's all behind the scenes.

1. The site loads a bit faster

Your site already loads pretty well — this isn't a rescue. But there was some unused code riding along and the homepage banner image was loading in a weird order. I trimmed the dead weight and made the banner load first since that's what visitors see first. Small stuff, but it adds up.

Why you care: Google uses page speed as a ranking factor. This isn't going to transform anything on its own, but it removes drag you don't need and gives you a little edge.

2. Google now sees your brand correctly

When Google looks at your site, it reads hidden data that tells it your brand name, your product details, your photos, and your URLs. Some of that data was wrong — it was sending "madman store" as your brand name, only showing Google one product photo instead of all of them, and using outdated URL formats. All fixed.

Why you care: This is what controls how your products look when someone Googles them. The product cards, the brand name, the images — all of that comes from this data. Now it's accurate.

3. Your site can now show up in richer ways on Google

I added two new features. First, breadcrumbs — a navigation trail like "Home > Products > Miami Watch" that appears on every page. Google can display this in search results instead of a raw URL. Second, your FAQ page is now tagged so Google can show your 8 questions and answers as expandable dropdowns right in the search results. Your listing takes up more space on the page, which means more clicks.

Why you care: A richer-looking search result gets more clicks than a plain blue link. These features make your listings stand out without paying for ads.

I also fixed some buttons that were too small to tap easily on phones and bumped up some text that was too small to read. Minor, but Google factors this into rankings and it makes the site feel better on mobile.

Bigger moves for clicks and conversions

Everything above is about making the site technically sound. The stuff below is about getting more people to actually click on your search results and buy once they land.

Get into Google Shopping

If you're not already in Google Merchant Center, this is probably the single biggest thing you could do. Google Shopping puts your product images, prices, and brand at the very top of search results — above the regular links. For a visual product like watches, that's where the clicks are. It's free to list products; you only pay if you run ads. Setting it up takes an afternoon.

Get review stars showing in search results

Judge.me is already on your site collecting reviews. Those ratings can show up as star ratings right in your Google search results — the gold stars under your link. If they're not showing up yet, check Judge.me's settings for "rich snippets." Stars in search results are one of the biggest click-through boosts there is. People trust a 4.8-star result over a plain link.

Test stronger SEO titles

Your current titles follow a good format: "Miami Watch - limited edition artist watch | Mad Hudson." But you could experiment with titles that create more curiosity. "Miami Watch — Only 100 Made" or "Miami Watch — Designed by Madison Hudson" lean into what makes your watches different: limited runs and named artists. The limited edition angle especially — scarcity catches eyes in a list of search results.

Show up in image search

People browse watches visually. Your main product images have good descriptions, but the lifestyle shots and extra angles are missing alt text (the descriptions that help Google understand what's in a photo). Filling those in means your watches can show up when someone does a Google image search for things like "square case watch" or "artist designed watch face." Image search is a real traffic source for visual products.

Own the "artist watch" keyword space

This is the biggest long-term opportunity. Right now, if someone searches "artist collaboration watches" or "watches designed by artists" or "limited edition art watches," there's almost nothing competing for those phrases. That's your territory. A few well-written blog posts about your artists — who they are, how the designs came together, what makes each watch a collaboration — could put Mad Hudson at the top of those results. This is the kind of content that compounds: one good post can drive traffic for years.
