Most ecommerce brands have nothing interesting to say. Mad Hudson has more stories than it knows what to do with — how you stumbled into making watches in the first place, why you care about the watch as an art object and not just a timepiece, who your artists are and how those collaborations happen. What to write about:

  • The origin story. You didn't set out to start a watch company — you stumbled into it. That's interesting. People love an accidental-founder story, and it makes the brand feel real in a market full of corporate pitch decks.
  • The artists. Who they are, how the collaborations happen, what the designs mean. You already tell these stories in person — put them on the site.
  • The watches as objects. Why you care about making something beautiful on its own terms, not just functional. The watch-as-art-piece angle is something almost no one in the space is talking about. That's your lane.
  • The craft and process. How a design goes from an artist's sketch to a finished watch. Behind-the-scenes content performs extremely well and builds the kind of trust that turns browsers into buyers.

Why it works for Mad Hudson specifically:

  • Search terms like "artist collaboration watches," "watches designed by artists," and "limited edition art watches" have almost zero competition. That's wide open territory.
  • Each post creates a new page that can rank on its own. Six artists + an origin story + a few process posts = a dozen chances to show up in search.
  • People who find you through a story are warmer leads than people who searched "buy watch." They already care about the thing that makes Mad Hudson different.
The compounding effect: A blog post written today drives traffic for years. Unlike ads, you don't pay per click. Unlike social, it doesn't disappear in 24 hours. One good post about how you accidentally started a watch company could become your top-performing page.
